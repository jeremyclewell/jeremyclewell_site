Congratulations. You have decided to run one of most exciting and fun 10-mile races in America. Whether this is your first or your fifteenth, this is a race that works better when you run with a plan. I am here to help you create and execute that plan.

The first part of your training requires that you use a watch. Using a watch while you run has its advantages. The first is when you begin a running program, you should never go for distance or speed: it should be time-based, meaning that your initial runs should be for a sustained time only. For example, a beginning runner training for a 5K race may have a goal of four miles a day. When you have never run, or are starting to run again after an extended period of time off, this is not a realistic goal. Using a watch on your runs and having a set time like 30 minutes per run is a better goal than trying to go for mileage. A watch has other advantages -- if you are running on a marked course, you can time your miles/laps and have an idea of your average pace. Another reason for wearing a watch is that for a run to benefit your body, you must do it for a minimum amount of time; wearing a watch can help you gauge your progress.

*LT pace: Lactate threshold pace is your 10K race pace per mile, plus about 20 seconds, or about 85% of your maximum heart rate (MHR). For beginners, this should be a comfortably hard, near race pace, with a steady, unvarying effort.

Meter Math

  • 400 meters = 1/4 mile
  • 200 meters = 1/8 mile
  • Most outdoor tracks are 400 meters.

Key terms for speed training

Threshold: This pace is just above easy and right below hard. It's the pace that you should be able maintain for 25 to 30 minutes.

Fartlek: An athletic training technique used especially in running in which periods of intense effort alternate with periods of less strenuous effort in a continuous workout.

"Interval: This training consists of running fast a number of times over short distances with a slow jog for recovery between the fast runs. During the fast runs, the body is at its VO2 max (when the body is working at it maximum oxygen consumption).
